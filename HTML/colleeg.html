Colleeg

How might we provide affordable help to high school students during the college admission process?

Statistically, more students from affluent homes get into more elite colleges‚Äîeven without cheating. This is because a lot of the stronger college applications are likely to have gone through expensive private counselors. Colleeg is a website that tries to make private guidance more affordable by helping high school students connect with university students.

Time

2 Weeks

Type

Personal Project

Skills

User Research
Interaction Design
User Testing
Visual Design

Problem

It is extremely difficult for high school students to finish college applications on their own. Meanwhile, private college counseling is highly inaccessible and ineffective for its price.

Challenge

How can we make a functioning service structure?
How can we differentiate this service from a plethora of other college application helpers?

Our Solution

A responsive web app that connects high schoolers with college student mentors, who can provide college admissions tips and counseling at a reasonable price.

View Interactive Prototype
SOLUTION OVERVIEW

1. Filters

Various layers of filters allow high schoolers to find their mentors more efficiently.

2. Schedule a Meeting

Students can schedule a meeting with a college student mentor of their choice. Scheduler makes students provide enough context about the meeting ahead of time, giving mentors enough time and information to prepare for the sessions.

3. Manage Meeting Sessions

Students can quickly access and enter upcoming meetings.

4. Send Feedback

Students can post reviews about their mentors. This helps the service manage the quality of individual meetings without monitoring them.

Background Research

Through background research, I found out that university admissions offices are not the only one to be blamed at. The applications themselves have a problem: the applications that appear stronger than the others are more likely to have gone through the help of a private counselor.

UNDERSTAND

Surveys & Interviews

I decided to take a deep dive into the topic of private college counseling. Are they effective? How are current high school students coping with college applications? To find out, I sent out a short survey to 8 high school students and interviewed 3 of them. I also talked to 3 college students who had private counselors in high school to gather more perspectives.

DEFINE

Competitive Analysis

I created a pro/con list of common features (1:1 help, forums, blogs, and articles) used by college counseling websites or career-related platforms. The goal was to analyze existing solutions/features and look for improvements.

DEFINE

Insights & Takeaways

Summary of insights gathered from competitive analysis, surveys, and interviews.

1. Students want honest opinion on their application

Students are eager to know their chances of getting into schools. They essentially want to get their essays, extracurriculars, portfolio, and resume critiqued by someone who can give an honest opinion.

2. Students want more comprehensive information

Students want to get more comprehensive, insider information about the programs they are applying to. However, this information is hard to obtain online.

3. Many students demand outside help with college applications

Most students acknowledge that they need some sort of help with college application. If their school counselors are not helpful enough, many students will seek help elsewhere with or without money.

4. Students found getting help from an upper-level student in college the most helpful.

Most students thought that it was ever more helpful than private counselors. However, most students found it challenging to ask for help due to shyness and ‚Äúsimply not being close enough.‚Äù

IDEATE

Brainstorm Solutions

The solution was a website where high school students can get 1:1 feedback about their application from an actual college student. The college students would have personal, candid, and helpful insights about college admissions and different programs/majors.

The new challenge was to find a way to make college students sign up.

To figure out how comfortable college students are with this idea, I interviewed several students from different universities.

Since incentive was an important motivational factor for college students, I envisioned an e-commerce service structure.

IDEATE

User Flow

I made a simple user flow before I jump into wireframes to gauge the scope of design. For the time being, I decided to skip the landing page and the login process.

DESIGN

High-fidelity Prototype

The user experience focuses on 4 things that my target users valued the most: personalization, efficiency, accessibility, and reliability.

Personalization

As shown in the solution overview, these various filters allow high schoolers to discover the type of mentor they need. Students can sort the mentors by their school, major, type of help they provide, grade, availability, and the number of reviews.

Efficiency

Note that the 'Meeting Availability' and the 'Response Rate' become particularly useful. They provide enough context for users to understand the mentor's schedule before clicking on the card.
The meeting planner makes students provide enough context about the meeting ahead of time. This allows mentors to prepare in advance and lead the sessions smoothly.

Accessibility

Ideally, students should be able to schedule meetings with a mentor of their choice (Ex: a mentor from a specific school studying a specific major). In real-life settings, schedules might not align, and that perfect mentor may not be available at the moment. The 'Request Schedule' feature prevents this kind of situation.

Reliability

Some people were concerned with the possibility of trolling or mentors being unhelpful. To prevent this, I added a review system. After each session, every user is encouraged to post a short feedback.
