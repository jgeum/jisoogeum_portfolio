Artie

How can we bring more attention to public artworks around your city? Artie is a mobile application that allows citizens to document their public art experience through 3D drawings. By creating their own version of public art and viewing other people's artworks, users can feel stronger civic pride and foster civic awareness and pride for local public artists and artworks, but also to bring the community together!

“How can public art in Pittsburgh foster civic engagement and pride?”

Our research participants perceived public art in Pittsburgh as "not interesting," "not engaging," and "not impressive." Our group wondered what we can do to alter this view and turn public art into one of Pittsburgh's strengths. Through further research, we found out that public art is very much related to civic engagement and pride.

Our Solution

Using location-based AR, our app enable users to engage with public art by documenting their reactions as virtual 3D artworks.

The Impact
1. Builds a tight-knit community driven by public art.
2. Raises awareness of public art and local artists.
3. Fosters civic pride.
4. Has potential to attract tourists, which brings positive economic impact to the area.

SOLUTION OVERVIEW

1. Create & Share Your Own Virtual Public Art

Users can document and share their creative reactions to a piece of public art. Features include customizable 3D line drawings, pre-made objects, and texts. One users are done with their artwork, they can decide whether they want the artwork to be seen by others or not. Through "View Mode," people can see and react to each other's artwork.

2. Discover New Public Art Around You

By clicking the animated "Find Art" button from the home screen, users can discover different public art pieces around them. Each detail page contains location information, basic information about the artwork, fun facts, and a "Support Artist" feature that allows artists to receive personal feedback and comments about their work. The feedback feature can be helpful for artists who want to engage with their viewers and improve their practice.

3. Discussion Board

When users are physically close to a public art, a fun question will pop up on the home screen. This feature creates an opportunity to build a community in which individuals can actively interact and get close to one another.

4. Notifications

Notifications are crucial in maintaining collective interactions between the users and the public art. Users will be notified when a friend creates a new artwork. This message can potentially motivate the users visit the public art in person to view their friend's virtual artwork. When users are close to a public art piece, another notification will pop up and encourage them to take a look. This feature ultimately increases public exposure of the artwork and supports local artists.

UNDERSTAND

Background Research

Why Does Public Art Matter?

Before we jump into the project, we wanted to narrow our scope. What exactly are we trying to solve? What do we want to focus on within this broad topic? What is so significant about public art?
To answer these questions, we conducted background research, focusing on the following criteria.
1. History of public art
2. Social, cultural, and economic benefits of public art
3. Technology used to measure audience engagement with art
4. Field Observation

The following information are the key points that helped us narrow our scope and focus on increasing civic engagement and pride through public art.

Economic Growth

Famous public landmarks in cities attract travelers and motivate locals to go outside. A continuation of this phenomenon ultimately leads to higher revenue for local businesses.

Increased attachment and pride for the city

High regards towards the public art within a city is directly related to increase in civic pride and attachment.

Stronger sense of community

Increased civic pride and cultural attachment also leads to a strong and vibrant community.

UNDERSTAND

Stakeholders & Users

Next, we wanted to have a better understanding of the people who are impacted by public art. Luckily, we found a similar research study called Metro 21’s Public Art + IoT = An Engaged Civic Space project. Through this, we were able to gain access to:
1. Interview footage from a Public Art & Civic Design manager in Pittsburgh
2. Pedestrian interviews
3. Usability-test recordings of Pittsburgh’s official public art website

With these information at hand, we were able to understand the pain points of people who are directly & indirectly impacted by public art. We also conducted a small interview with a local artist and a restaurant owner to hypothesize various stakeholder personas and customer journey map.

DEFINE

Interviews

Now, it was time for us to conduct research that is more focused to our goal:

What do people think about public art in Pittsburgh?

Does public art impact how the citizens of Pittsburgh view their city?  If so, are they positive or negative? How about the travelers? Is public art one of the motives for visiting Pittsburgh? If not, can public art become an unexpected encounter that creates a meaningful experience? — these were some of the questions we had in mind.
To gain more insights on how people view public art in Pittsburgh, we interviewed 5 pedestrians in Pittsburgh. 3 of them turned out to be visitors.

Based on our insights, our team generated a few ideas that would aim to increase civic engagement with public art. Solution

Our solution uses location-based AR, which enables users to engage with public art in real-time. Users can engage with the artwork and the people who have seen the artwork by expressing their reactions through 3D drawings and texts. The app also provides opportunity for users to discover more public art around the area and learn about the artist's intent. The social media aspect of the application allows people to communicate with friends as well as strangers. This solution not only helps residents build civic pride and communal interests, but also helps local artists and businesses.

DESIGN & TEST

Experience Prototype

Our first prototype was a low fidelity paper-experience prototype. We layered paper-made buttons on top of an existing augmented reality app that lets users virtually draw three-dimensional lines in space (Just A Line app).

Before user testing, we prototyped a sign that would be placed near the art to invite people to use the augmented reality app. It showcases the possibilities of what users would do before they actually walk over and engage with the app.

DESIGN & TEST

Think-Aloud Protocol

To test the need for our service, we decided to use the think-aloud protocol method. To increase our chances of getting interviewees, we created a sign that said “Explore Augmented Reality,” and we placed it in front of Dippy the Dinosaur. In this case, the dinosaur is the public art, and if a passerby showed interest, we would then approach and ask if they would like to test out our low-fidelity app.

DESIGN & TEST

UI Design

Based on the feedback from the user-testing participants, we made some changes to our service. We decided that a location-based push notification feature would be a great digital replacement for the signs that are placed near public art. We also took more consideration into the general usability of the app as well as some controversial elements such as hate/inappropriate comments.
The next steps were wireframing, med & hi-fi prototyping, and usability testing. For this step of the process, I had to work on my own as this course project initially began and ended as a case study.

Final Insights & Takeaways

Although we initially focused on a very specific problem out of the three criteria that impact the livability of a city - increased sense of community and civic pride towards public artworks - we realized that the product has a potential to improve the other areas as well.

Economic growth
Does the art attract travelers? With gained popularity of the “AR public art phenomenon”, more travelers will visit Pittsburgh to experience this location-sensitive experience.

‍Attachment and cultural identity
Can the artworks define and represent Pittsburgh city, individuals, and communities? The fact that residents have creatively contributed to the space will increase the pedestrian’s emotional attachment. The exhibited artworks of individuals and communities will not only increase the engagement within each other but also represent those individual and communities. As a city that is very technologically driven, the AR experience will amply represent the city of Pittsburgh.

Cultural understanding
Can the viewers understand and relate to the artworks in Pittsburgh? The “Spin The Wheel” activity will tell the users interesting facts about specific artworks and invite them to respond to the facts using the AR app. This activity itself will increase the user’s cultural understanding and relatability to the artwork.
