Artie

How can we bring more attention to public artworks in Pittsburgh?

Artie is a social application that allows citizens to draw and share their own 3D art as a response to an existing public art. When users upload their 3D objects nearby a physical art piece, the objects are placed in a virtual space, which can be accessed through the camera view of the application. By encouraging people to embrace and document local public art, Artie generates a stronger sense of civic pride and fosters awareness for local artists.

My Role

This was originally a 3 month-long collaborative research project, which ended with a product idea proposal. After the end of this collaborative research, I decided to make our group's idea come true by prototyping the mobile app.

SOLUTION OVERVIEW

1. Create & Share Your Own Virtual Public Art

Users can document and share their creative reactions to a piece of public art. Features include customizable 3D line drawings, pre-made objects, and texts. One users are done with their artwork, they can decide whether they want the artwork to be seen by others or not. Through "View Mode," people can see and react to each other's artwork.

2. Discover New Public Art Around You

By clicking the animated "Find Art" button from the home screen, users can discover different public art pieces around them. Each detail page contains location information, basic information about the artwork, fun facts, and a "Support Artist" feature that allows artists to receive personal feedback and comments about their work. The feedback feature can be helpful for artists who want to engage with their viewers and improve their practice.

3. Discussion Board

When users are physically close to a public art, a fun question will pop up on the home screen. This feature creates an opportunity to build a community in which individuals can actively interact and get close to one another.

4. Notifications

Notifications are crucial in maintaining collective interactions between the users and the public art. Users will be notified when a friend creates a new artwork. This message can potentially motivate the users visit the public art in person to view their friend's virtual artwork. When users are close to a public art piece, another notification will pop up and encourage them to take a look. This feature ultimately increases public exposure of the artwork and supports local artists.

UNDERSTAND

Background Research

Why Does Public Art Matter?

We blindly jumped into the project by asking this question first. Why does it matter so much in our lives? Is there a problem? What do we want to focus on within this broad topic?

To answer these questions, we conducted background research, focusing on the following criteria.
1. History of public art
2. Social, cultural, and economic benefits of public art
3. Technology used to measure audience engagement with art
4. Field Observation

Key Points

The following are the key points that helped us narrow our scope and focus on increasing civic engagement and pride through public art.

Economic Growth

Famous public landmarks in cities attract travelers and motivate locals to go outside. A continuation of this phenomenon ultimately leads to higher revenue for local businesses.

Increased attachment and pride for the city

High regards towards the public art within a city is directly related to increase in civic pride and attachment.

Stronger sense of community

Increased civic pride and cultural attachment also leads to a strong and vibrant community.

Solution

Our solution uses location-based AR, which enables users to engage with public art in real-time. Users can engage with the artwork and the people who have seen the artwork by expressing their reactions through 3D drawings and texts. The app also provides opportunity for users to discover more public art around the area and learn about the artist's intent. The social media aspect of the application allows people to communicate with friends as well as strangers. This solution not only helps residents build civic pride and communal interests, but also helps local artists and businesses.

DESIGN & TEST

Experience Prototype

Our first prototype was a low fidelity paper-experience prototype. We layered paper-made buttons on top of an existing augmented reality app that lets users virtually draw three-dimensional lines in space (Just A Line app).

Before user testing, we prototyped a sign that would be placed near the art to invite people to use the augmented reality app. It showcases the possibilities of what users would do before they actually walk over and engage with the app.

DESIGN & TEST

UI Design

Based on the feedback from the user-testing participants, we made some changes to our service. We decided that a location-based push notification feature would be a great digital replacement for the signs that are placed near public art. We also took more consideration into the general usability of the app as well as some controversial elements such as hate/inappropriate comments.
The next steps were wireframing, med & hi-fi prototyping, and usability testing. For this step of the process, I had to work on my own as this course project initially began and ended as a case study.
